# Next steps — Colorado Environmental Data Hub

This is the human handoff memo for Colorado Environmental Data Hub, the shareable companion to the `ROADMAP.md` checklist. Read it to learn what was just built, what to do next, and who owns it, without opening a tracker or a board. The assignable, checkbox version of everything below lives in `ROADMAP.md`; this page exists so a PI or stakeholder can stay oriented in a couple of minutes.

We scaffolded Colorado Environmental Data Hub at level **L1**, deliberately right-sized to the team and horizon that exist now rather than to a project we imagine later. What that gives you is ready to use today: the repository structure, the documentation, and a documented 56-source catalog are in place, so you can clone the repo, find your way around from the `README.md`, and start working against real data. Building only this much now — and recording the rest as deferred work — is the intended workflow, not a corner cut.

The top next actions, and who owns them, are tracked as an assignable checklist in `ROADMAP.md` under "This level's work," and as GitHub Issues #2–#5. Treat that table as the live to-do list and check items off as their definition-of-done is met. Default ownership sits with **Brian Keegan (PI, @brianckeegan)** unless a row names someone else — the two `good-first-issue` data-quality tasks (#2 verify the `needs_followup` sources, #3 quarantine the spurious NREL `nlr.gov` claim) are intentionally left unassigned for incoming undergraduate contributors to claim. The short version for a reader who will not open the table: harden the catalog (verify the flagged sources, kill the poisoned NREL domain, add the `match_hosts`/`match_keywords` citation-match fields), then stand up the L2 reproducible pipeline that `context/architecture.md` already specifies.

A few items are marked **blocking** in `ROADMAP.md`, and they gate real actions — relying on unverified sources, running Stage-A citation detection, or climbing to the next level — so resolve them, or explicitly accept the risk in writing, before doing the thing they guard. The future news corpus also carries blocking copyright/fair-use and journalist-privacy duties (see the roadmap), which must be satisfied before any article text or journalist records are ingested. Do not route around a blocking item quietly; raising it here is how a stakeholder learns the project is paused for a good reason.

To climb to the next level, re-run the `data-project` skill in this directory and ask for the next rung (for example, "take this to L2"); it adds only the new level's artifacts, refreshes `ROADMAP.md`, files the new level's tasks as issues, and leaves what already works untouched. Climbing is how scope grows on purpose instead of by accident, so prefer it to hand-adding heavyweight structure this level does not yet need.

These tasks are also filed as GitHub Issues and organized on the **Colorado Environmental Data Hub** Project board so the team can assign and track them in the open — see `PROJECT-MANAGEMENT.md` for how that mirror works and how to keep it in sync. The `ROADMAP.md` checklist stays the source of truth; GitHub is a projection of it, so edit the checklist and re-sync rather than letting the two drift.
